# Food Diary Strategy

## Target problem

Tracking food intake on paper doesn't give instant feedback on daily calorie and macro totals, making it hard to stay consistent when working toward a weight goal. Commercial apps solve the tracking problem but trade simplicity and data ownership for subscriptions and features you didn't ask for.

## Our approach

Build only what's actually needed and nothing more — a minimal, exact-fit tool that stays useful without growing into something its author wouldn't want to use. Every feature decision starts with: do I actually need this?

## Who it's for

**Primary:** The author and open-source community members with similar needs — they're hiring Food Diary to replace paper notes and commercial apps with a lightweight, self-hosted tracker that fits their exact habits.

## Key metrics

- **Daily personal use** — app is actively used for logging meals and weight; measured by personal habit
- **GitHub stars / forks** — community interest and adoption signal; measured on GitHub
- **Community contributions** — self-hosters filing issues and opening PRs; measured on GitHub

## Tracks

### AI-assisted logging

Make food entry faster via photo recognition and AI — reduce the friction of logging meals.

_Why it serves the approach:_ Faster logging means the app stays part of the daily habit without becoming a chore.

### Core tracking features

Nutrition (calories, macros, sugar, salt), weight logging, and personal food database — the fundamentals that make the app worth opening.

_Why it serves the approach:_ These are the exact features the author needs; keeping them sharp is the whole point.

### Dev experience / modernization

Keep the stack current, improve self-hosting setup, and maintain developer ergonomics.

_Why it serves the approach:_ A personal project only survives if working on it stays enjoyable and friction-free.

## Not working on

- Social and sharing features — this is a personal tracker, not a community platform
